Week 2 — Thursday, January 22
What is Design?! — Complexity and Context

This lecture reframed design as something far bigger than making things look good. Designers navigate competing conditions, constraints, ideas, values, and criteria particular to a situation, and then configure the relationships between them.

Key definition: "Devise courses of action aimed at changing existing situations into preferred ones." — Herbert Simon, The Sciences of the Artificial (1969). This definition is powerful because it doesn't discipline design outcomes, doesn't prescribe what type of action is needed, introduces "preferred" rather than just "change," and shifts focus from the object of design to the situation it changes.

The lecture explored how design exists in context — whether we are aware of it or not. What are the consequences of not being aware of the contexts that shape design? Design is both an Action (a process, the act of designing) and a Thing (a result, artifact, plan — an object). When we talk about "objects" in this course, it means anything: architecture, print, literary works, ideas, interactions.

The Plastic Water Bottle Case Study

What problem did it solve? Convenience, commercializing PET plastic, selling a nation's water supply back to them. What problems did it create? 17 million barrels of oil annually, only 23% recycled, 500+ year decomposition into microplastics contaminating the food chain, corporate water-rights displacing communities. Nestle pays $15/day for water rights, sells it globally. This exercise shows how a simple "design solution" creates vast chains of unseen consequences.

The Ergonomic Chair Thought Experiment

If asked to design a chair comfortable for 10 hours — what situation are you condoning? What other courses of action might address the real situation (labour rights, workplace health)?

The class generated a word cloud of "What Shapes Design?" — everything from economics, politics, privilege, ego, demand, access, ignorance, empathy, culture, ideology, war, zeitgeist, ethics, and more. In other words: CONTEXT.

Week 3 — Thursday, January 29
Ontological Propositions — The Ecologies of Technology (Part I)

This week introduced the idea that technology is never neutral. Francis Bacon wrote in 1620 that printing, gunpowder, and the compass changed the world more than any empire, sect, or star. But depending on your frame of reference, these technologies could represent progress or tools of colonial indoctrination, conquering, and exploitation.

Core argument: Ontology = we make things and things make us. Things are not neutral; they act on us, afford certain behaviours, enable actions and exclude others. They co-shape us. Technology transforms our social frameworks, forms of life, human wants and possibilities, and the experience of our bodies, nature, and the planet.
"Sustainability is more than finding ecologically rational methods of production and consumption; it also involves collective judgment on those patterns... As a normative concept sustainability is a political/ethical issue first and, only derivatively, a technical/economic one second." — John Barry
"I consider questions about sustainability to be essentially normative and any answers they prompt essentially contested... For to suggest there is a need for technologies that are sustaining is to beg the question, sustaining of what?" — Aidan Davison

The recurring question: "What do we want to sustain?" Sustainability is a political/ethical issue first, a technical one second. We need to decide what kind of world we want before we can judge our solutions.

Week 5 — Thursday, February 12
Shadow Constitutions — The Ecologies of Technology (Part II)

Building directly on Week 3, this lecture introduced Langdon Winner's concept of Shadow Constitutions: technologies structure our lives like laws do, but unlike laws, they aren't publicly debated or voted on.

"Consciously or unconsciously, deliberately or inadvertently, societies choose structures for technologies that influence how people are going to work, communicate, travel, consume and so forth for a very long time." — Langdon Winner, The Whale and the Reactor (1986)
Key frameworks introduced:
Causal vs. Meaningful impacts: We exist in the world both causally (physical reality, natural laws, physical impact) and meaningfully (social worlds, cultural expectations, societal norms and values).

Technology directs its force disproportionately away from the user (Andrew Feenberg) — the hunter feels a slight pressure on their shoulder as a bullet strikes the rabbit; a driver hears a faint rustling of leaves as they hurtle a ton of steel down the highway. The convenience of use obscures the wider impact.

Reciprocity: "Only God can act on his objects without reciprocity" (Feenberg). As humans in a finite world, everything is reciprocal — nothing goes into the world without effect on us.

Secondary Impacts — Causal and Meaningful

Beyond a technology's immediate benefit, what are the secondary impacts? The lecture used the Alberta oil sands as an example of secondary causal impacts (the largest industrial project in history, spanning two provinces, clearing forest, removing "overburden" which is industry speak for topsoil/subsoil/clay).

Barry Commoner's 4 Laws of Ecology (1971)

1. Everything Is Connected To Everything Else — one ecosphere, what affects one affects all.
2. Everything Must Go Somewhere — no "waste" in nature, no "away" to throw things.
3. Nature Knows Best — technological improvements on nature are likely detrimental.
4. There Is No Such Thing As A Free Lunch — every technological gain has a cost, all debts are eventually paid.
"It matters what matters we use to think other matters with; it matters what stories we tell to tell other stories with... It matters what worlds make stories, what stories make worlds." — Donna J. Haraway, Staying with the Trouble
"Does it matter whether we write with pencils or on an iPad? Whether we engage in activities collectively in the neighbourhood or in the solitude of our individual rooms in nuclear homes? In what ways do these diverse practices construct different selves and societies? Does it matter?" — Arturo Escobar, Designs for the Pluriverse

Week 7 — Thursday, February 26
Sustainability(s) — A Different Kind of Material Culture?

This is the lecture from your Week 5 slides file. It reframed sustainability not as an environmental issue but as a social issue.

Core argument: Climate change is the end result of all human activity — it is the wrong place to start looking. Unsustainability is manifest through the mundane decisions people make every day: getting dressed, eating, getting around, communicating, making life a little easier. These innocuous decisions tie us to a great chain of relations, seen and unseen. These are exactly the place and scale at which design intervenes in the world.

Climate change is the aggregate effect of billions of people passively attending to their own convenience, comfort, and/or survival. "Passive" is an important word — existing or experiencing without active or concerted effort. The ability to be passive, to not care, is a kind of luxury born of comfort and privilege.

The distinction between "a good life" vs. "the good life" — can we invite people to be active participants in the choices that shape our world instead of passive consumers?

"Climate Change is not an environmental issue. Climate Change is a social issue." When we say social, we also mean societal. Linking individual and societal is one of the big transitions we need in sustainability.

Design intervenes in spaces of personal agency, spaces of choice, at the level of behaviour and action, and in spaces of thinking. These are powerful places for systemic shifts to begin.

The lecture covered the "Design Sleepwalking" research, mapping roles and strategies for sustainability and systemic change. In-class activity: S.L.O. (Secret Life of...) Object Pitches.

Part 2: Key Readings & Viewings

Required Readings

"Bodies Tumbled into Bodies" by Anna Tsing (PDF on Moodle) — explores entanglement, multispecies encounters, and how bodies (human and non-human) are always already mixed up with each other. Key for understanding ecological interconnection.

"Why We Need New Stories on Climate" by Rebecca Solnit (PDF on Moodle) — argues that the climate crisis is also a crisis of narrative. We need new stories that move beyond doom/despair and open up possibility for collective action.

"Maple Nation: A Citizenship Guide" by Robin Wall Kimmerer (from Braiding Sweetgrass) — Kimmerer reimagines citizenship through the lens of maple trees. The maples are the most generous citizens of the community — providing shade, firewood, syrup, habitat, carbon storage — all without being asked. She proposes a "Maple Nation Bill of Responsibilities" instead of a Bill of Rights, arguing for reciprocity with the natural world. Citizenship means giving back, not just paying taxes. Carbon is the currency of Maple Nation: "traded, bartered, among community members from atmosphere to tree to beetle to woodpecker to fungus to log to firewood to atmosphere and back to tree. No waste, shared wealth, balance, and reciprocity."

"Home" by Dr. Zoe Todd — a short, accessible story about what it means to be home on the land. Grounding for the Zoe Todd talk.

"Goodbye Snauq" by Lee Maracle — video on how we work with story (available on Moodle).

"What is Care at Planetary Dimensions?" by Patricia Reed (PDF on Moodle) — explores care beyond the individual scale.

Anatomy of an AI System — Kate Crawford and Vladan Joler's investigation mapping all the human labour, data, and planetary resources required to build and operate an Amazon Echo. A key text for understanding the hidden ecologies of technology.

Part 3: Key Vocabulary & Concepts

Epistemology — How we know what we know; the study of knowledge and justified belief.

Ontology — The nature of relation and being. "We make things and things make us." Things are not neutral; they co-shape us.

Normative — Ideas about what we "ought" to do. Rooted in values and ways of seeing the world. Whose values? Whose right and wrong?

Shadow Constitutions (Langdon Winner) — Technologies act as unwritten laws, structuring society without public debate.

Design Sleepwalking (George Orwell / Langdon Winner) — How we unconsciously reconstitute the conditions of human existence through our relationship with technology.

Reciprocity — In a finite world, everything is reciprocal. Nothing goes into the world without effect. Central to Indigenous worldviews (Kimmerer, Simpson).

Defuturing — Design decisions that close down future possibilities and ways of being.

Pluriverse (Arturo Escobar) — "A world where many worlds fit." Honouring multiple ways of being and knowing, rather than one dominant worldview.

Transition Design — Design aimed at societal transitions toward more sustainable futures.

Commoning / Recommoning — Reclaiming shared resources essential to community survival.

Bio-region — A geographic area defined by ecological systems rather than political boundaries.

Aggregate Effect — How billions of small mundane decisions collectively produce massive systemic outcomes like climate change.

Human Exceptionalism — The belief that humans are fundamentally separate from and superior to nature.

Animacy / Agency of Objects — The idea (central to many Indigenous worldviews) that objects and non-human beings have life, spirit, and capacity to act.

Part 5: Your Secret Life of... Film Project

The Secret Life of Protein Powder

Team: Vitoria, Keren, Carmen, Candela, Max

Your claim: Protein powder is not inherently bad — it's a genuine nutritional tool for specific populations. The critique is the system built around it: an unregulated industry that manufactured mass protein anxiety in a population that largely doesn't need supplementation. The commodity has become an identity project — what you supplement with signals body ideals, tribe, and class position.

Three Time Periods:

Period 1 (Late 1800s-Early 1900s): Plasmon — a dried milk product for malnourished hospital patients. Invisible to ordinary people, shaped by medical need.

Period 2 (1950s-1990s): The supplement industry co-opts bodybuilding's steroid-fuelled results, attributes them to powder, and builds an empire on fiction — creating the gym and the post-workout shake as new social spaces and rituals.

Period 3 (2000s-Present): Protein anxiety, manufactured by decades of marketing, sells a solution to a problem most people don't have — while the plastic tub, Instagram selfie, and shaker cup complete the transformation of food into a data point.

Key Research Points:

The regulatory void (classified as neither food nor medicine), contamination issues (half of tested products contain lead/cadmium/arsenic), the Weider Research Clinic fiction, the shaker cup as identity object, most early "gains" were from steroids not supplements, and how the industry creates the need it claims to fill.

Remaining Project Steps:
